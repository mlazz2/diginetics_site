Smoke Shop Point-of-Sale Analysis

The same engine that analyzed MLB players was pointed at real retail data with zero code changes — only a different configuration file. Here's what it found.

About this data: 180 days of real daily point-of-sale data (August 2025 – February 2026) from a smoke shop with 15 product departments. 98 metrics tracked: baskets (transactions), items sold, dollar sales, average sale price, and scan rate for each department plus store-wide totals. "Backtest accuracy" means the equation's predictions beat last-value and mean-value baselines on held-out time windows. R² indicates how much variance in the target metric is explained by the discovered equation.

Data Used

Daily aggregated POS data across 15 departments: legal THC, glass, hookah, vape devices, disposable vapes, coils/pods, incense, cigarillos, cigarettes, drinks, Zyn pouches, tobacco, whip-its, legal mushrooms, and store totals. For each department: daily basket count, items sold, dollar sales, average sale price, and scan rate. The system was configured to discover cross-department relationships and time-lagged effects with no prior knowledge of the business.

Cross-Department Causal Dependencies

The most valuable findings were relationships between departments that no standard dashboard or BI tool would reveal. These are mathematical equations — not correlations.

Cross-Sell Discovery

Vape device transactions depend on cigarillo traffic

vape_devices_baskets = vape_devices_items - 2 × (vape_devices_items / cigarillos_baskets)   (R² = 0.985)

Vape device transaction volume is mathematically dependent on cigarillo basket traffic. R² of 0.985 means this equation explains 98.5% of the variance. This suggests co-locating these departments and running joint promotions — cigarillo customers are vape device buyers.

Hidden Dependency

Cigarette sales are driven by vape + cigarillo interaction, not cigarette demand

cigarettes_baskets = (disp_vape_items × 0.0036 × cigarillos_baskets) + 1   (R² = 0.710)

Cigarette transactions aren't self-driven. They depend on the interaction between disposable vape volume and cigarillo traffic. If vape supply drops due to a shipping delay or regulation, cigarette baskets will decline — even if cigarette inventory is fully stocked. R² of 0.710 means this explains about 71% of the variance.

Impulse Category

Drink sales are pure impulse — driven by store traffic, not drink demand

drinks_baskets = (3 × total_items) / scan_rate   (R² = 0.603)

Drink sales have nothing to do with drink-specific factors. They're a function of total store volume divided by scan efficiency. More people moving through slowly = more impulse purchases. Drink-specific promotions won't move the needle — increasing overall foot traffic will.

Purchasing Pattern

Glass pieces are a 1:1 category — don't bundle

glass_baskets = (glass_items / 1.1473) + 0.2527   (R² = 0.955)

Customers buy approximately 1.15 glass items per transaction. This ratio is stable across 180 days (R² = 95.5%). "Buy 2 get 1" deals won't work here — customer behavior doesn't support volume purchasing. Focus on per-unit margin and upselling to premium tiers instead.

Isolated Department

Hookah is self-contained — doesn't drive or respond to other departments

hookah_baskets = hookah_items / avg_items_per_basket   (R² = 0.771)

Hookah transactions are entirely explained by hookah item demand. Hookah customers buy hookah products and leave — they don't cross-shop. Hookah promotions won't drive traffic to other departments. If hookah underperforms, the fix is hookah-specific.

Time-Lagged Feedback Loops

The system discovered causal relationships with specific time delays — telling you not just what drives what, but how long the effect takes to appear.

Disposable vape avg sale is a 3-day early warning for store health

disp_vape_avg_sale → total store avg_sale (strength: +1.22, lag: 3 days)

When disposable vape average sale rises, the entire store's average sale follows 3 days later. Track this metric daily as an early warning system for store-wide revenue trends.

Glass average sale pulls the entire store upward with a 10-day lag

glass_avg_sale → total store avg_sale (strength: +2.72, lag: 10 days)

This is the strongest positive feedback loop for store-wide average sale — 2.2x stronger than the disposable vape signal. Investing in premium glass pieces doesn't just improve glass department revenue — it measurably lifts the store's overall average transaction value.

Cigarette-only traffic drags down store economics

cigarettes_baskets → total store avg_sale (strength: -0.71, lag: 10 days)

More cigarette-only transactions lower the store's average sale 10 days later. Cigarette customers are low-value, single-purpose shoppers who don't cross-buy. If cigarette basket share is growing, counter by promoting cross-sell items at checkout.

Backtest Methodology

Rolling-window backtests with strict temporal ordering. No lookahead, no data leakage. Each equation tested against last-value and mean-value baselines on held-out windows. 485 of 490 equation-window combinations passed (99.4%). The 5 that failed were in low-variance metrics where both the equation and baselines performed similarly.
