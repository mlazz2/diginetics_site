Key Insight: Different Players, Different Drivers

The system doesn't apply the same model to every player. It discovers what each player actually optimizes, producing a unique causal map per player while preserving universal baseball physics (e.g., barrel rate drives slugging for everyone).

Aaron Judge — Power Hitter

Plate discipline is the primary driver of power production

chase_rate → exit_velocity (sensitivity: -9.52)

For Judge, a 1% reduction in chase rate corresponds to a ~9.5 mph increase in exit velocity. The system identified plate discipline — not swing mechanics — as the causal upstream driver of his power output. This is a sensitivity measurement from the system's Greek analysis, not a guaranteed outcome.

Mookie Betts — All-Around Hitter

Launch angle management is the primary driver of outcomes

popup_pct → launch_angle (sensitivity: +54.15)
gb_pct → launch_angle (sensitivity: -48.61)

For Betts, the system found that batted ball distribution — specifically managing popup rate and ground ball rate — is the dominant causal lever. A completely different optimization strategy than Judge, discovered automatically from the same engine.

Universal — Both Players

Barrel rate drives outcomes regardless of player type

barrel_pct → slg (+1.37)  |  barrel_pct → hr (+1.10)  |  hr_fb_pct → hr (+1.24)

These relationships were consistent across both Judge and Betts, reflecting fundamental baseball physics that the system correctly preserves while adapting player-specific strategies.

Lindor Deep Dive: Causal Feedback Loops

The Lindor analysis (3,068 plate appearances, 9 MLB seasons) produced the most detailed results. The system discovered 263 time-lagged feedback loops — relationships where a change in one metric causes a measurable change in another metric after a specific number of plate appearances.

Swing decisions change what pitchers throw him

pitches_in_zone = 1 + (zone_swings / 1.5748)   (R² = 0.523)

Every additional zone swing generates approximately 0.63 additional in-zone pitches. This is a discovered equation with R² of 0.523, meaning it explains about 52% of the variance in pitches received in the zone. The remaining variance comes from pitcher strategy, game context, and other factors not captured by this single equation.

Pitches per PA is the leading indicator for performance shifts

pitch_number_max → rolling_50_hit_rate (strength: -19.58, lag: 10 PAs)

The strongest feedback loop in the Lindor system: longer at-bats predict lower hit rate with a 10-PA delay. But longer at-bats also predict higher walk rate. The system quantifies this tradeoff — it's not just "patience is good" or "patience is bad," it's a measurable tradeoff with specific magnitudes.

Score context changes power output measurably

away_score → rolling_50_HR_rate (strength: -25.13, lag: 3 PAs)

When the opposing team has a higher score, Lindor's HR rate drops within 3 PAs. This is the fastest-acting feedback loop related to power production in the entire system — detected automatically from raw Statcast data without any scouting input.

Backtest Methodology

Every equation is validated through rolling-window backtests with strict temporal ordering. The system trains on historical data, then tests predictions on future windows the model has never seen. Each equation is compared against two baselines: last-value (naive persistence) and mean-value (historical average). An equation "passes" if it outperforms both baselines across the majority of test windows. The 99.3% figure means 276 out of 286 equation-window combinations passed this test for Lindor. The 10 that failed were primarily noisy short-window metrics where signal is inherently weak.

What these numbers mean: UPS analyzed 180 days of daily point-of-sale data (August 2025 – February 2026) from a real smoke shop with 15 product departments. "Equation discovery" means the system found a valid mathematical relationship for that metric. Cross-Department Causal Dependencies

The most valuable findings were relationships between departments that are invisible to standard dashboards. These aren't correlations — they're mathematical equations showing how one department's activity drives another's.

Cross-Sell Discovery

Vape device transactions depend on cigarillo traffic

vape_devices_baskets = vape_devices_items - 2 × (vape_devices_items / cigarillos_baskets)   (R² = 0.985)

Vape device transaction volume is mathematically dependent on cigarillo basket traffic. When cigarillo baskets increase, vape device baskets adjust upward. The R² of 0.985 means this equation explains 98.5% of the variance in vape device basket counts. This suggests co-locating these departments and running joint promotions.

Hidden Dependency

Cigarette sales are driven by vape + cigarillo interaction

cigarettes_baskets = (disp_vape_items × 0.0036 × cigarillos_baskets) + 1   (R² = 0.710)

Cigarette transactions aren't driven purely by cigarette demand. They're driven by the interaction between disposable vape volume and cigarillo traffic. If vape supply drops due to a shipping delay or regulation, cigarette baskets will decline even if cigarette inventory is fully stocked. R² of 0.710 means this explains about 71% of the variance.

Impulse Category

Drink sales are pure impulse — driven by store traffic, not drink demand

drinks_baskets = (3 × total_items) / scan_rate   (R² = 0.603)

Drink sales have nothing to do with drink-specific factors. They're a function of total store volume divided by scan efficiency. More people moving through slowly = more impulse drink purchases. This means drink-specific promotions won't move the needle — increasing overall foot traffic will. R² of 0.603 means this explains about 60% of the variance.

Purchasing Pattern

Glass pieces are a 1:1 category — don't bundle

glass_baskets = (glass_items / 1.1473) + 0.2527   (R² = 0.955)

Customers buy approximately 1.15 glass items per transaction. This ratio is stable across 180 days (R² = 95.5%). Glass is not a cross-sell category. "Buy 2 get 1" deals won't work here — the customer behavior doesn't support volume purchasing. Focus on per-unit margin and quality tier upselling instead.

Time-Lagged Feedback Loops

The system discovered causal relationships with specific time delays — telling you not just what drives what, but how long the effect takes.

Disposable vape average sale is a 3-day leading indicator for store health

disp_vape_avg_sale → total store avg_sale (strength: +1.22, lag: 3 days)

When disposable vape average sale rises, the entire store's average sale follows 3 days later. This is the fastest feedback loop in the dataset. Track this metric daily as an early warning system for store-wide revenue trends.

Glass average sale pulls the entire store upward

glass_avg_sale → total store avg_sale (strength: +2.72, lag: 10 days)

Glass price increases boost the entire store's average sale 10 days later. This is 2.2x stronger than the disposable vape signal. Investing in premium glass pieces doesn't just improve glass department revenue — it measurably improves the store's overall average transaction value.

Cigarette-only traffic drags down store economics

cigarettes_baskets → total store avg_sale (strength: -0.71, lag: 10 days)

More cigarette-only transactions lower the store's average sale 10 days later. Cigarette customers are low-value, single-purpose shoppers who don't cross-buy. If cigarette basket share is growing relative to other departments, expect store-wide avg sale to decline. Counter by promoting cross-sell items at checkout.
