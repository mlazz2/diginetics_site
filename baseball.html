About this data: All analysis used real MLB Statcast data. "Backtest accuracy" means the percentage of discovered equations that outperformed baseline methods (last-value and mean-value) on held-out time windows the model never saw during training. "Equation discovery" means the system found a valid, non-trivial mathematical relationship for that metric. Data Used

Full career Statcast data — 1,152 games across 10 MLB seasons. 48 metrics organized in a 3-layer causal hierarchy: Layer 1 (14 decision/approach metrics like swing rate, chase rate, contact rate), Layer 2 (17 contact quality metrics like exit velocity, barrel rate, launch angle), Layer 3 (17 outcome metrics like HR, BA, OBP, SLG, wOBA). The system discovers how changes in Layer 1 propagate through Layer 2 to drive Layer 3 outcomes.

Key Findings

Primary Discovery

Plate discipline drives power production — not swing mechanics

chase_rate → exit_velocity (sensitivity: -9.52)

For every 1% reduction in chase rate (swinging at pitches outside the zone), Judge's exit velocity increases by approximately 9.5 mph. The system identified plate discipline — not bat speed or swing path — as the dominant upstream driver of his power. This is a sensitivity measurement, meaning it quantifies the rate of change between these metrics across his career data.

Contact Quality Chain

Zone contact vs. outside contact have opposite effects

z_contact_pct → exit_velocity (+2.06)  |  o_contact_pct → exit_velocity (-2.44)

Making contact on pitches in the zone increases exit velocity. Making contact on pitches outside the zone decreases it. The magnitudes are similar but the directions are opposite — confirming that for Judge, selectivity matters more than raw contact ability.

Universal Physics

Barrel rate drives outcomes (consistent across all players)

barrel_pct → slg (+1.37)  |  barrel_pct → hr (+1.10)  |  hr_fb_pct → hr (+1.24)

These relationships are consistent across every player the system has analyzed. They represent fundamental baseball physics: barreling the ball leads to power outcomes. The system correctly preserves these universal truths while discovering player-specific strategies above.

Only 1 metric classified as weak: Triples

47 of 48 metrics produced strong equations. The only weak metric was triples — a highly volatile, speed-dependent stat that represents a tiny fraction of Judge's profile (he averages ~2 triples per season). The system correctly identified this as noise rather than forcing a false pattern.

Data Used

Full career Statcast data — 3,068 plate appearances across 9 MLB seasons. 58 metrics in a rates configuration: pitch counts (balls, strikes, pitches seen), plate discipline (zone swings, chases, whiffs, fouls), contact quality (exit velocity, launch angle), game context (score, outs, runners), and rolling performance rates at 5, 10, 20, 50, and 100 PA windows for hit rate, K rate, BB rate, HR rate, exit velocity, barrel rate, and hard hit rate.

Key Findings

Master Variable

Pitches per PA is the single best leading indicator for performance shifts

pitches_seen = pitch_number_max + constant   (R² = 0.999)

Across 263 feedback loops, pitches_seen and pitch_number_max appear more frequently than any other metric. Changes in at-bat length precede changes in output metrics by 3–10 plate appearances. A coaching staff can monitor one number — pitches per PA — and get advance warning of performance shifts before they show up in traditional stats.

Pitcher-Batter Feedback

Lindor's swing decisions change what pitchers throw him

pitches_in_zone = 1 + (zone_swings / 1.5748)   (R² = 0.523)

Every additional zone swing generates approximately 0.63 additional in-zone pitches. More zone swings → pitchers throw more in the zone → more hittable pitches. The R² of 0.523 means this equation explains about 52% of the variance — the remaining 48% comes from pitcher strategy, game context, and other factors.

The Deep Count Paradox

Longer at-bats predict lower hit rate but higher walk rate

pitch_number_max → rolling_50_hit_rate (strength: -19.58, lag: 10 PAs)
pitches_in_zone → rolling_20_HR_rate (strength: +5.64, lag: 3 PAs)

The system quantified Lindor's central tradeoff: working deep counts brings harder velocity and more walks but lower batting average. Being aggressive early on zone pitches spikes HR rate but walks disappear. The equations give exact magnitudes so a coaching staff can decide when each approach is optimal based on game situation.

Context Effects

Score context changes power output within 3 PAs

away_score → rolling_50_HR_rate (strength: -25.13, lag: 3 PAs)

When the opposing team has a higher score, Lindor's HR rate drops within 3 PAs. This is the fastest-acting feedback loop in the entire system — detected automatically from raw Statcast data without any scouting input.

Backtest Methodology

Every equation was validated through rolling-window backtests with strict temporal ordering. The system trains on historical data, then tests predictions on future windows the model has never seen. Each equation is compared against two baselines: last-value (naive persistence) and mean-value (historical average). The 99.3% figure means 276 out of 286 equation-window combinations passed. The 10 that failed were primarily noisy short-window metrics where signal is inherently weak.

Data Used

Full career Statcast data — 1,521 games across 10 MLB seasons. Same 48-metric configuration as Judge, allowing direct comparison between player profiles. The system ran the identical pipeline to determine whether different player types produce different causal structures.

Key Findings

Primary Discovery

Launch angle management is Betts' primary driver — not plate discipline

popup_pct → launch_angle (sensitivity: +54.15)
gb_pct → launch_angle (sensitivity: -48.61)

For Betts, the system found that batted ball distribution — specifically managing popup rate and ground ball rate — is the dominant causal lever for outcomes. This is a completely different optimization strategy than Judge's plate discipline focus. The system discovered this automatically from the same engine, proving it adapts to player profiles.

Contact Quality

Hard hit rate drives both exit velocity and power

hard_hit_pct → exit_velocity (+21.93)  |  hard_hit_pct → ev_50 (+25.82)

For Betts, hard hit percentage has a much stronger relationship to exit velocity than it does for Judge. The sensitivity of +21.93 means each percentage point of hard hit rate corresponds to a ~22 mph change in exit velocity — roughly double the magnitude of Judge's equivalent relationship. Betts' value chain runs through contact quality more than selectivity.
